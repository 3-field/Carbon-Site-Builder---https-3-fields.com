The table salt is harvested by Saxa from fields in Cheshire, the brine pumped to the surface and processed to remove impurities, transferred to factories in Worksop and Kent (47 and 259 miles respectively from where I presently sit) where it is then evaporated in a vacuum process, dried, and crushed and screened before being packaged with Sodium Hexacyanoferrate II (to prevent caking). One [[sea salt]] is 'produced' in the same place, using a natural evaporation process. A process which is less efficient, and takes much more time and labour. Saxa admits to sourcing much of their salt from British Salts, a company that has produced salts for the chemical industry, road safety and medical grade salts since 1969. British Salts is part of Tata Chemicals Europe, which is owned by Tata Group, an Indian multinational conglomerate founded in 1868 headquartered in Mumbai. The other [[sea salt]] is harvested from the Blackwater Estuary River in Maldon, Essex, 227 miles from where I presently sit, using a natural evaporation process that has changed little in 140 years (this is my most expensive salt). The [[kala namak]] (black salt) and [[pink salt]] are both from the Himalayas (about 4900 miles from where I presently sit).
